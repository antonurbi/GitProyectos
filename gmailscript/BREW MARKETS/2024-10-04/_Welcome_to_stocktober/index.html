GEOPOLITICS

Getting defensive

Francis Scialabba

Geopolitical conflict in the Middle East boiled over this week, and a war between Israel and Iran looks increasingly likely.

One logical result of this terrible news is that defense stocks are rising. Investors are anticipating that the escalating conflict in the Middle East means more defense spending around the globe.

The S&P Aerospace & Defense Select Industry Index leaped 2% over the past five trading days after Iran fired missiles at Israel and Israel launched a ground invasion into Lebanon, even as the S&P 500 sank 1% during the same time period.

Shares of defense firms Lockheed Martin and RTX jumped to all-time highs on Tuesday, while Northrop Grumman and L3Harris reached their highest share prices in the past two years, as investors brace for the bleak reality that more violence may lie ahead.

Should you invest now?

Founder of Political Analysis at Pangaea Policy Advisory Terry Haines recommends that investors take this time to reposition themselves in response to what he calls the “highest geopolitical risk in over 50 years,” in a Tuesday note.

He argued that the defense sector could still rise, in spite of its gains over the past week. “The possibility of something snapping and geopolitical risk jumping even higher is not completely priced in,” he said during a CNBC appearance yesterday.

Haines also noted that investors aren’t anticipating the extent to which a new president will need to shore up defense capabilities, because defense spending hasn’t been discussed heavily on the campaign trail. No matter which political party takes over the White House in November, investors can expect writing up bigger defense contracts to be one of the first orders of business, Haines explained.

Zoom out…The hope is that a diplomatic solution can be reached in the upcoming weeks. If that happens, short-term defense demand may drop—even if today’s conflict points to further fighting in the years ahead.

But not everyone is as bullish about long-term defense demand. “For U.S. defense, the whole sector has become increasingly expensive relative to a growth outlook that currently looks likely to peak in 2025,” wrote Deutsche Bank analyst Scott Deuschle in a note today. “And while multiples can perhaps continue to rise given the current geopolitical backdrop, that's not something we're fully comfortable calling for.” — LB

BY THE NUMBERS

Stat of the day

Alain Jocard/Getty Images

Talk about a bad investment: Elon Musk paid $44 billion for Twitter (or X, if you’re lame) and took it private in late October 2022. It is now worth $9.4 billion — an 80% decline in just under 2 years.

It’s tough to figure out exactly how much a private company like X is worth, but there are hints. Every quarter, Fidelity’s Blue Chip Growth Fund files a Form N-Q with the SEC detailing the fund’s complete portfolio holdings. One of those is X Holdings Corp. Class A shares—aka, what used to be Twitter shares—which account for a mere 0.006% of the fund.

As of September 29, Fidelity values that stake at just under $4.2 million, which is well below the $19.66 million those shares were worth back in October 2022. Some back-of-the-napkin math tells us that pegs Fidelity’s estimate of the total value of X at about $9.4 billion.

Now, that’s just Fidelity’s best guess—other X investors may value the company differently. But considering the social media company’s struggles to keep both users and advertisers ever since Musk bought it, X’s value is a lot lower than it once was, no matter who’s running the numbers.

SPOOKY SEASON

October's ominous history

Timothy A. Clary/Getty Images

It’s officially October, which means that jack-o’-lanterns are gracing porches, scary movies are playing all month, and it’s time to pretend you recognize your most intellectual friend’s obscure literary Halloween costume (of course you’re the The Bleeding Nun from Matthew Lewis’s The Monk, published in 1796).

But nothing is spookier than a market crash.

October’s volatile history has long haunted investors. Historically, nine of the 20 largest single-day percentage declines in the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) occurred this month—the most notorious of the bunch including the Stock Market Crash of 1929, Black Monday in 1987, and huge drops during the 2008 financial crisis.

And wouldn’t you know it, we’ve already kicked off October with the S&P 500 dropping 1% after ascending to record highs during the first three quarters of the year.

It’s not just the eerie history of this month that has investors spooked. We’re on the precipice of a high-stakes US presidential election, rising geopolitical tensions in the Middle East, and we’re currently knee-deep in the biggest dockworker strike since 1977.

Don’t get too superstitious

The biggest market downturns always seem to happen in October, but there really isn’t much evidence that we’re more likely to experience a crash this month than any other.

In fact, analysts are largely bullish on the spooky season, even if we experience some bumps on the road upward. Goldman Sachs’ Scott Rubner is even worried his year-end 6,000 price target is too low. He expects a late October rebound, despite a slower start to the month.

Why the optimism? The Fed’s 50 basis point haircut should go a long way to energizing the economy like an exhilarated 4-year-old after eating an entire bag of candy corn.

“Lower interest rates are likely to fuel a rebound of hiring in credit-intensive industries like housing, manufacturing, and retailing of big-ticket consumer products like cars, furniture, and appliances,” wrote Chief Economist for Comerica Bank Bill Adams in an October economic outlook.

Wall Street is also optimistic about corporate earnings—according to FactSet, analysts are projecting corporate profits to surge 15% through the fourth quarter of 2024 and first quarter of 2025.

So maybe the real ghost this year is the bear market, which is nowhere to be found alive right now. — LB

CALENDAR

Tomorrow’s the big one. The monthly US Jobs Report has become a must-watch event for investors ever since the July report tanked the market. The lower-than-expected employment numbers that month spooked investors, making them think that the Fed had missed the boat on curtailing a recession, sending stocks tumbling.

Thankfully, the August report was stronger, with 142,000 jobs added (far better than the 114,000 in July), and a solid unemployment rate of 4.2%. It was good enough to allay fears of an economic downturn, but investors are still keeping a close eye on the labor market—particularly since the Fed has made it clear that employment will pay a key role in determining the size and pace of rate cuts.

Economists expect tomorrow’s September report to show that employers added 140,000 jobs—lower than August, but not bad at all—while unemployment should remain steady at 4.2%. Any lower than that, and the stock market’s shaky start to October could get very worse very quickly.
